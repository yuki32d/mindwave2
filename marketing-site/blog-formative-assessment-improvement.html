Formative Assessment: The Key to Continuous Improvement

📅 April 2025⏱ 8 min read🏷 Formative Assessment · Feedback

Educational assessment is often divided into two categories using a famous culinary analogy by Robert Stake: "When the cook tastes the soup, that's formative. When the guests taste the soup, that's summative."

In higher education, we spend vast amounts of time and resources serving soup to the guests. Midterms, final exams, and major term papers are summative assessments. They measure learning at the end of an instructional period to assign a grade. They are evaluative, high-stakes, and final.

The problem? If the soup is ruined, finding out at the dinner table is too late to fix it. This is why a pedagogical shift toward formative assessment is one of the most powerful changes a faculty member can make.

What is Formative Assessment?

Formative assessment is the ongoing process of gathering data about student understanding while learning is still happening, with the explicit goal of adjusting instruction to improve outcomes. It is diagnostic, low-stakes, and iterative.

Crucially, formative assessment is not a type of test; it is a function of a test. A quiz is only formative if the results are actually used by the instructor to change what happens next, and by the student to change how they study.

The Problem with the Summative Trap

Relying solely on summative exams creates a brittle learning environment. Students navigate weeks of instruction with only a vague sense of their own competence (often suffering from the illusion of fluency). When the exam arrives, anxiety is high. If they perform poorly, the class moves on to the next topic anyway. The gap in their knowledge becomes a permanent foundation for future failure.

Implementing Formative Assessment Effectively

Effective formative assessment doesn't require doubling your grading workload. In fact, the best formative assessments are often ungraded or auto-graded. Here are high-impact strategies:

1. The Minute Paper / Muddiest Point

In the last two minutes of class, ask students to write down: "What was the most important thing you learned today?" and "What is still confusing (the muddiest point)?" Reading these responses takes ten minutes and will completely dictate how you start the next class.

2. Live Classroom Polling

Interrupt a lecture every 15 minutes with a conceptual multiple-choice question. If 80% get it right, move on. If 40% get it right, you know instantly that your explanation failed, and you must try a different approach immediately. This prevents a localized point of confusion from compounding into a lost lecture.

3. Low-Stakes Practice Quizzes

Offer weekly, auto-graded quizzes that count for a negligible portion of the final grade (e.g., 5% total). These force students to practice retrieving information regularly, leveraging the spacing effect, while giving them a safe environment to fail and calibrate their understanding.

4. Peer Review as Formative Feedback

Having students review each other's drafts against a rubric before the final submission provides them with immediate feedback while simultaneously forcing the reviewer to internalize the criteria for success.

The Cultural Shift

Moving toward formative assessment requires a cultural shift in the classroom. Students have been conditioned to view all assessment as punitive evaluation. Faculty must explicitly communicate the purpose of formative activities: "This poll is not for a grade. It is for me to see if I did a good job explaining this, and for you to see if you understood it. It is okay to be wrong here."

When assessment transforms from an autopsy of failed learning into a diagnostic tool for continuous improvement, the adversarial relationship between testing and teaching dissolves. Testing simply becomes a part of learning.
