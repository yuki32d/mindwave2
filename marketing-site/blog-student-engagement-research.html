Student Engagement in Higher Education: What the Research Says

📅 March 2025⏱ 8 min read🏷 Engagement · Learning Science

The word "engagement" appears in practically every university's strategic plan, every faculty development programme, and every edtech marketing deck. It has become so ubiquitous that it risks losing its meaning. But beneath the jargon, the concept points to something genuinely important and genuinely difficult to achieve: the state in which a student is cognitively, emotionally, and behaviourally invested in their own learning.

The data on how often that state is achieved in higher education is sobering. A Gallup study of 30,000 college students found that only 36% were actively engaged — attentive, curious, and energised in their classes. The majority — 45% — were "not engaged," going through the motions. A further 16% were actively disengaged, potentially disrupting the learning environment for others.

Three Types of Engagement

Behavioural Engagement

The observable dimension: attendance, participation, time on task, completion of assignments. This is what most institutions measure — and it is the easiest to measure poorly. A student who is physically present and has submitted all assessments is behaviourally engaged. Whether they are actually learning is a separate question.

Cognitive Engagement

The mental dimension: the depth of processing a student applies to learning tasks. A student who takes notes by copying slides verbatim is cognitively disengaged; a student who is connecting new information to prior knowledge, generating examples, and asking "why does this work?" is cognitively engaged.

Emotional Engagement

The affective dimension: the sense of belonging, interest, and positive identification with an institution or course. Students who feel anonymous in large lectures, who have no meaningful relationship with faculty, or who see their education as purely instrumental are emotionally disengaged.

What the Research Shows Actually Works

A comprehensive meta-analysis reviewed interventions designed to improve student engagement. The most effective included:

  • Active Learning Structures: Replacing a portion of passive lecture with activities requiring student response — polling, group discussion, problem-solving.
  • Instructor Immediacy: Faculty behaviours that communicate warmth, approachability, and genuine interest in student success.
  • Frequent Formative Assessment: Regular low-stakes quizzes, participation activities, or check-ins.
  • Relevance Framing: Connecting course content to real-world applications and career outcomes.
